IRAC - the framework for everything

Every exam answer, memo, and in-class response runs on IRAC. Master this before day one.

I

Issue

Identify the precise legal question the facts raise. Phrase it specifically - not “Is there a contract?” but “Did a valid contract form when Smith emailed acceptance three days after the offer?” The more specific your issue, the better.

R

Rule

State the governing law completely and accurately, without tying it to the facts yet. Example: “A contract requires offer, acceptance, and consideration. Acceptance must be communicated before the offer lapses or is revoked.” You must know rules cold - this is what you memorize.

A

Analysis / Application

This is where you earn your points. Apply the rule to the specific facts. Use signal phrases: “Here...” or “In this case, because...” Professors reward thorough analysis even when your conclusion is wrong. Address borderline issues - they’re included intentionally.

C

Conclusion

One to two sentences. Directly answer the issue. The conclusion is the least important section if your analysis is strong. “Therefore, a valid contract was formed when Smith sent the acceptance email.”

IRAC worked example - negligence (Torts)

Fact pattern: Marcus owns a coffee shop. On a rainy Tuesday morning, a customer tracked water onto the tile floor near the entrance. A wet puddle formed and was visible to shop employees for approximately 25 minutes before Elena, a customer, slipped on it, fell, and fractured her wrist. No warning sign was placed near the puddle. Marcus argues he was busy with the morning rush and did not have time to address it. Elena sues Marcus for negligence.
I Issue Spot the legal question

Whether Marcus, the owner of a coffee shop, is liable to Elena for negligence when a puddle was visible to his employees for 25 minutes before she slipped and fractured her wrist, and no warning was provided.

Why it’s written this way: The issue is specific - it names the parties, flags the legally significant facts (25 minutes, no warning, visible to employees), and frames the precise question. A vague issue like “Was Marcus negligent?” earns almost nothing. Specificity shows you already understand what the law will care about.
R Rule State the law - no facts yet

To establish negligence, a plaintiff must prove four elements: (1) the defendant owed a duty of care to the plaintiff; (2) the defendant breached that duty; (3) the breach was the actual and proximate cause of the plaintiff’s injury; and (4) the plaintiff suffered damages.

A business owner owes a duty of reasonable care to invitees - those who enter the premises for a business purpose. Breach occurs when the owner knew or should have known of a hazardous condition and failed to remedy it or warn of it within a reasonable time.

Why it’s written this way: The rule is stated in pure blackletter law - no mention of Marcus, Elena, or the puddle. You are telling the professor: “I know the doctrine.” The second paragraph adds the specific invitee/premises liability sub-rule because that’s what will drive the analysis. Always include the sub-rule that applies to your specific issue.
A Analysis This is where points are earned

Working through each element:

Duty: Elena entered Marcus’s coffee shop as a paying customer, making her a business invitee. Marcus therefore owed her a duty of reasonable care to maintain the premises in a reasonably safe condition.
Breach: Here, the puddle was visible to Marcus’s employees for approximately 25 minutes before Elena fell. A court would likely find that 25 minutes is sufficient time for a reasonably careful business owner to notice the hazard, place a warning sign, or clean it up. Marcus’s argument that the morning rush prevented action does not negate the breach - the standard is objective reasonableness, not personal convenience. The absence of any warning sign further supports a finding of breach.
Causation: The puddle was the actual cause of Elena’s fall - but for the unaddressed hazard, she would not have slipped. It is also the proximate cause: it is entirely foreseeable that a customer walking near a wet entrance on a rainy morning would slip if no warning was given.
Damages: Elena suffered a fractured wrist - a concrete, documentable physical injury. This satisfies the damages element.
Why it’s written this way: Every element of the rule is addressed in order. The word “Here” signals the transition from rule to application. The breach section is the longest because that’s where the real dispute lives. Marcus’s counterargument is acknowledged and refuted - professors reward students who engage with the strongest counterargument rather than ignoring it.
C Conclusion Brief - one or two sentences

Therefore, Marcus is likely liable to Elena for negligence. All four elements are satisfied: he owed her a duty as a business invitee, breached it by failing to address a known hazard for an unreasonable amount of time, that breach caused her fall, and she suffered actual damages.

Why it’s written this way: The conclusion is short and direct. It answers the issue and briefly ties back to the elements - but does not re-argue anything. Notice “likely” - epistemic humility is appropriate because reasonable people could disagree on breach. Absolute certainty can cost you points if the professor sees the issue as close.
Grading takeaway: The analysis section - especially breach - is where an A separates from a B. Raising and refuting the “morning rush” defense, citing the objective standard, and flagging the missing warning sign all show issue-spotting depth. Total length is roughly one page, which is the target for a single-issue IRAC on an exam.

Case brief structure

1Case name + citation - parties and legal reporter. Tracks the exact opinion.
2Procedural history - how the dispute arrived here. Who won at trial? Who appealed?
3Facts - the events causing the conflict. Only include facts that affect the legal outcome.
4Issue - the exact legal question, phrased as a yes/no question.
5Rule - the law or legal test the court applies. Written in general terms, no facts mixed in.
6Reasoning / analysis - how the court applies the rule to the facts. Professors target this section during Socratic cold calls.
7Holding - the court’s final decision. This is the “C” in IRAC.
8Concurrences + dissents - judges who agreed via different logic, or disagreed entirely. Professors use these for policy discussion.
Target: half a page to one page per brief. Anything longer becomes unusable in real-time class discussion. Full briefing is a 1L training tool - by 2L most students book-brief or skip it entirely.

July - Torts & Contracts (weeks 1-4)

Torts

Palsgraf v. Long Island Railroad Co.

New York Court of Appeals, 1928

The single most famous Torts case you will read. A railroad employee’s negligent act caused a chain reaction that injured a bystander. Judge Cardozo’s majority opinion established the “zone of danger” test for proximate cause: a defendant owes a duty only to foreseeable plaintiffs. Justice Andrews’s dissent argues for a broader view of liability. You will be cold-called on both opinions.

Contracts

Carlill v. Carbolic Smoke Ball Co.

Court of Appeal, 1892

A company advertises that anyone who uses their product and still gets the flu will receive £100. A woman buys it, uses it, gets the flu, and sues. The company claims the ad was mere puffery. The court holds it was a binding offer accepted by performance. This case defines what constitutes an offer, the nature of acceptance, and consideration in one clean ruling. Arguably the most entertaining case you’ll read all semester.

New York Court of Appeals, 1891

An uncle promises his nephew $5,000 if he refrains from drinking, tobacco, swearing, and card-playing until age 21. The nephew complies. The uncle dies without paying. The court holds that the nephew’s forbearance of a legal right constitutes valid consideration. This is the foundational American case on consideration as forbearance - you will cite it constantly.

Hadley v. Baxendale

Court of Exchequer, 1854

A mill owner hires a carrier to deliver a broken crankshaft for repair. The carrier delays. The mill owner sues for lost profits. The court holds that consequential damages are only recoverable if they were foreseeable to both parties at the time of contracting - establishing the cornerstone rule of contract damages that still governs today. Every Contracts student memorizes the phrase “arising naturally or in contemplation of both parties.”

August - Civil Procedure, Criminal Law & Property (weeks 1-3, before orientation)

Civil Procedure

Pennoyer v. Neff

U.S. Supreme Court, 1877

The foundational case on personal jurisdiction. A man is sued in Oregon while living in California. The court sets out the territorial theory of jurisdiction: a state court can only haul someone in if they are physically present in the state or have property there. This case is dense and old, but it establishes the baseline that every subsequent jurisdiction case reacts to. Read it slowly - your professor will assume you struggled with it, which is part of why they assign it.

Personal jurisdictionDue process

Civil Procedure

International Shoe Co. v. Washington

U.S. Supreme Court, 1945

Read this immediately after Pennoyer. A shoe company with traveling salespeople in Washington refuses to pay state unemployment taxes. The Supreme Court replaces Pennoyer’s rigid territorial test with the modern “minimum contacts” standard: a defendant may be sued wherever they have sufficient contacts with the state such that jurisdiction would not offend “traditional notions of fair play and substantial justice.” This phrase will come up in every Civ Pro class for months.

Criminal Law

Regina v. Dudley and Stephens

Queen’s Bench Division, 1884

Four shipwrecked sailors are stranded at sea. After weeks without food, three of them kill and eat the weakest - a 17-year-old orphan - to survive. When rescued, two are tried for murder. They argue necessity. The court rejects it and convicts. This case is the foundation of the necessity defense in criminal law and establishes that necessity cannot justify killing an innocent person. The facts are unforgettable. The moral questions will carry into class discussion.

People v. Conley

Illinois Appellate Court, 1989

A teenager hits another with a wine bottle at a party, causing permanent nerve damage. The issue is whether the defendant acted with the specific intent required for aggravated battery. The court examines how intent is inferred from conduct and circumstance, not mind-reading. This is the go-to case for understanding the mental state (mens rea) requirement - a concept that runs through every criminal law issue you will encounter.

Property

Pierson v. Post

New York Supreme Court, 1805

A fox hunter chases a fox across open land. A stranger swoops in and kills it. Who owns the fox? The court rules that pursuit alone does not create possession - you need actual capture or mortal wounding. This sounds trivial until you realize it opens every question in property law: what does it mean to “own” something, and how is ownership established? The dissent argues for the hunter. Know both arguments.

Hawkins v. McGee

New Hampshire Supreme Court, 1929

A doctor promises to give a patient a “100% perfect hand” via skin graft surgery. The surgery goes wrong - badly. The patient is left with a hand covered in chest hair. The court awards expectation damages: the difference between what was promised and what was delivered. This is technically a Contracts case, but it appears in Property and Contracts courses alike, and it appears in “The Paper Chase,” the definitive law school film you should watch before August.

Pre-semester prep - what to do now

Build reading stamina

Read complex nonfiction for 2+ hours daily without distraction. Law school assigns hundreds of pages of dense text weekly. Your brain needs to be conditioned to retain on a single pass.

Read actual opinions

Use the reading list above. Start with Vosburg, Carlill, and Pierson v. Post - short, readable, foundational. Graduate to Palsgraf and Pennoyer by late July.

Improve typing speed

Your exam grade depends on how much structured analysis you can type in 3 hours. Target 70+ WPM consistently. Use keybr.com or typingclub.com before August.

Build your calendar system now

Map out your weeks before orientation. Block coaching shifts at Brooklyn Boulders, flag exam windows (Dec 2-16), and build in reading blocks. Automation removes decision fatigue during the semester.

Communicate your schedule early

Give your employer your exam dates - Dec 2-16 - as early as possible. Surprises during finals are manageable conflicts you don’t need.

Access BLS resources early

Log into BLSConnect and Canvas before classes start. Activate Core Grammar for Lawyers with your professor’s class code. Check the 1L library guide at guides.brooklaw.edu/1l.
