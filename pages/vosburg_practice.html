Fact pattern

Jordan is a thirteen-year-old student at Riverdale Middle School. During a supervised free period, students are required to remain at their desks while the teacher steps briefly into the hallway. The classroom rules are in effect — students must stay seated and keep noise to a minimum. The period is considered part of the formal school day.

Marcus, also thirteen, sits directly behind Jordan. Frustrated after losing a card game to Jordan at lunch, Marcus reaches forward and flicks Jordan's right ear with his finger. Marcus intends to flick Jordan. He does not intend to cause any real injury — only a momentary sting.

What neither boy knows is that Jordan has a previously undiagnosed benign cyst located in his right inner ear canal. The flick transmits vibration directly into the ear canal. Over the next 48 hours, Jordan experiences worsening pain and is taken to a physician. The physician diagnoses rupture of the cyst, causing permanent partial hearing loss in Jordan's right ear. The physician confirms that the same flick would have caused no lasting harm to a person without the pre-existing condition, but that Jordan's ear was highly susceptible to exactly this type of force.

Jordan's parents bring a battery claim against Marcus. Marcus's attorney raises two defenses: (1) Marcus did not intend to harm Jordan, only to annoy him briefly; and (2) the permanent hearing loss was not a foreseeable result of a finger flick, and therefore Marcus should not be liable for it.

Instructions: Using Vosburg v. Putney as your controlling authority, analyze Marcus's liability to Jordan for battery. Apply IRAC structure. Address both defenses raised by Marcus's attorney directly and separately. Your answer should be approximately 400–600 words. Do not assume any facts not stated above.

Model answer

Issue

Whether Marcus is liable in battery to Jordan when he intentionally flicked Jordan's ear during a formal school period without intending injury, and Jordan suffered permanent hearing loss due to a pre-existing condition that made him abnormally susceptible to harm from that contact.

Rule

Battery requires an intentional, unlawful touching of another person. The intent required is the intent to perform the physical act — not the intent to cause harm. Where the act itself is unlawful, the absence of malicious intent is no defense.

Under the unlawful act doctrine from Vosburg v. Putney, a touching occurring in a context where no implied license for such conduct exists is unlawful as a matter of law. The Vosburg court drew a precise distinction: a kick among schoolchildren during recess or play may carry an implied license, but the same kick during an active school session does not, because it violates the order and decorum the setting demands.

As to damages, the eggshell plaintiff rule holds that once an unlawful act is established, the defendant is liable for all injuries flowing directly from that act — whether or not those injuries were foreseeable. The Vosburg court stated this rule explicitly: the wrongdoer "is liable for all injuries resulting directly from the wrongful act, whether they could or could not have been foreseen by him." The defendant takes the plaintiff as he finds him.

Analysis

Defense 1 — No intent to harm: Marcus contends that because he intended only minor annoyance and not real injury, he cannot be held liable for battery. This defense fails under Vosburg. The court confronted the identical argument when Putney's counsel argued that the absence of evil intent defeated the claim. The court rejected it: the intent required for battery is the intent to perform the unlawful act, not the intent to produce the specific harm. Here, Marcus plainly and deliberately reached forward and flicked Jordan. That volitional contact is sufficient. The fact that Marcus hoped to cause only mild irritation does not convert his deliberate act into an accident.

Defense 2 — Unforeseeability of the injury: Marcus's second argument — that the permanent hearing loss was not a foreseeable result of a finger flick — also fails, though for a distinct reason. This defense confuses two separate questions: whether the act was unlawful, and what damages follow from an unlawful act. Foreseeability of the specific injury is irrelevant to the damages analysis once an unlawful act is established. The eggshell plaintiff rule directly controls. Jordan had a pre-existing condition that made him abnormally vulnerable to precisely this type of contact. Marcus did not know this. Under Vosburg, that ignorance provides no shelter: Marcus took Jordan as he found him. The permanent hearing loss flowed directly from the flick, satisfying the causation requirement. The unforeseeability of the severity of the harm does not limit liability.

The unlawfulness of the act: The remaining predicate question is whether the flick was unlawful in the first place. Here, the Vosburg framework maps precisely onto the facts. The court distinguished playground conduct — where implied license among schoolchildren might excuse contact — from conduct during a formal school session, where no such license operates. The free period in this case is not a recess or a break from school authority. Students were required to remain seated, maintain quiet, and follow classroom rules. The setting is materially identical to the Vosburg classroom. No implied license for physical contact existed. Marcus's flick was therefore an unlawful act regardless of his intent.

Conclusion

Marcus is liable to Jordan for battery. His deliberate contact satisfies the intent element — intent to harm is not required. Because the flick occurred during a formal school period governed by behavioral rules, no implied license existed and the act was unlawful. Under the eggshell plaintiff rule from Vosburg, Marcus is liable for all direct injuries resulting from that unlawful act, including Jordan's permanent hearing loss, regardless of whether such injury was foreseeable.

Grading rubric — 100 points

10 pts Issue statement: Identifies that the central question is battery without intent to harm, and that both defenses — intent and foreseeability — are in dispute. A vague issue ("Is Marcus liable?") earns 4 pts maximum.
15 pts Rule — battery and unlawful act doctrine: States that intent to act (not intent to harm) is sufficient. Correctly articulates the unlawful act doctrine: the setting, not the defendant's purpose, determines whether implied license exists. The playground/classroom distinction from Vosburg must be named.
15 pts Rule — eggshell plaintiff: States the rule accurately and ties it to Vosburg's language. Defendant liable for all direct injuries "whether they could or could not have been foreseen." Paraphrasing acceptable; the substance must be precise.
20 pts Analysis — Defense 1 (no intent to harm): Applies Vosburg to show that deliberate physical contact satisfies intent, and that intent to cause the specific injury is not required. Must reference Putney's identical failed defense in the original case.
20 pts Analysis — Defense 2 (unforeseeability): Applies the eggshell rule and explains clearly why foreseeability of the specific injury is irrelevant once an unlawful act is established. Addresses Jordan's pre-existing condition directly.
10 pts The setting distinction: Explicitly connects the supervised free period to Vosburg's formal-school-session holding. Identifies that behavioral rules were in effect and no implied license existed. This is the pivotal factual move the question tests — many students miss it.
10 pts Conclusion and legal writing: Conclusion directly answers the issue. IRAC structure maintained throughout. Signal phrases used to transition from rule to application ("Here," "In this case"). Clear, committed position — this fact pattern is not close; excessive hedging costs points.
What separates an A from a B: Most students correctly identify the eggshell plaintiff rule. The move that earns an A is catching the implied license / setting argument — recognizing that the supervised free period with behavioral rules removes any license that might otherwise exist among schoolchildren. If you got both defenses and the setting distinction, you wrote an A answer.
Common mistakes to check against: (1) Framing the issue as whether Marcus "intended to harm" Jordan — intent to harm is not an element of battery. (2) Treating foreseeability as relevant to the eggshell plaintiff analysis — once the act is unlawful and causation is met, the severity of injury need not be foreseeable. (3) Missing the setting entirely and analyzing the flick as if it occurred on a playground.
